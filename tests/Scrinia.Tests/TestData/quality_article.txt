The Human Brain and the Central Nervous System

The human brain is the most complex organ in the known universe. It serves as the command center of the central nervous system, coordinating the activities of virtually every system in the body. The central nervous system consists of the brain and the spinal cord, and together these structures process sensory information, regulate motor functions, and give rise to consciousness, emotion, memory, and cognition. The peripheral nervous system extends from the central nervous system to reach every tissue and organ in the body, forming a communication network of extraordinary complexity.

The brain itself is divided into several major regions, each responsible for distinct functions. The cerebral cortex, the outermost layer of the brain, is responsible for higher cognitive functions including language, reasoning, planning, and voluntary movement. The cerebral cortex is organized into four lobes on each hemisphere: the frontal lobe, the parietal lobe, the temporal lobe, and the occipital lobe. The frontal lobe of the cerebral cortex is particularly expanded in humans relative to other primates, a fact that many neuroscientists associate with the human capacity for complex social behavior, executive function, and abstract reasoning.

Beneath the cerebral cortex lies a collection of structures known collectively as the subcortical structures. These include the basal ganglia, the thalamus, the hypothalamus, the amygdala, and the hippocampus. The basal ganglia play a crucial role in motor control and learning, and dysfunction of the basal ganglia is implicated in movement disorders such as Parkinson's disease and Huntington's disease. The thalamus functions as a relay station, routing sensory information from the peripheral nervous system to the appropriate regions of the cerebral cortex. The hypothalamus regulates fundamental physiological processes including body temperature, hunger, thirst, sleep, and the release of hormones from the pituitary gland.

The amygdala is involved in processing emotional responses, particularly fear and anxiety. Research using functional neuroimaging has shown that the amygdala is activated by emotionally salient stimuli, and studies of patients with amygdala damage suggest that the amygdala is essential for the formation of emotional memories and for the recognition of fearful facial expressions. The hippocampus, located in the medial temporal lobe, is critical for the formation and consolidation of new declarative memories. The hippocampus converts information from short-term memory into long-term memory through a process called memory consolidation, which involves the repeated reactivation and strengthening of neural connections during sleep.

The cerebellum, situated at the base of the brain behind the brainstem, plays an essential role in motor coordination, balance, and the learning of motor skills. Although the cerebellum accounts for only about ten percent of the brain's total volume, it contains approximately half of all neurons in the brain. The cerebellum receives input from the sensory systems, the spinal cord, and other parts of the brain, and it integrates this information to fine-tune motor commands issued by the cerebral cortex. Damage to the cerebellum typically produces ataxia, a condition characterized by uncoordinated and clumsy movements, as well as difficulties with balance and gait.

The brainstem, which connects the cerebral hemispheres to the spinal cord, controls the most fundamental life-sustaining functions of the central nervous system, including heart rate, breathing, blood pressure, and consciousness. The brainstem consists of three main structures: the midbrain, the pons, and the medulla oblongata. The reticular formation, a network of neurons distributed throughout the brainstem, plays a key role in regulating arousal and consciousness. Lesions of the reticular formation can result in coma, demonstrating the central importance of the brainstem to maintaining wakeful consciousness.

Neurons are the fundamental cellular units of the nervous system. A typical neuron consists of a cell body, or soma, which contains the nucleus and the primary metabolic machinery of the cell. Extending from the soma are two types of processes: dendrites and axons. Dendrites are typically branching, tree-like structures that receive incoming signals from other neurons and convey them toward the cell body. Axons are usually longer and less branching than dendrites, and they conduct electrical signals away from the soma to other neurons, muscles, or glands. Many axons in the central nervous system and peripheral nervous system are wrapped in a myelin sheath, a fatty insulating layer produced by oligodendrocytes in the central nervous system and by Schwann cells in the peripheral nervous system.

The myelin sheath increases the speed at which electrical signals travel along the axon through a mechanism called saltatory conduction. In myelinated axons, the electrical signal does not propagate continuously along the axon membrane but instead jumps from one node of Ranvier to the next, where the myelin sheath is interrupted. This jumping of the signal from node to node greatly increases conduction velocity and reduces the metabolic cost of signal transmission. Diseases that damage the myelin sheath, such as multiple sclerosis, disrupt this rapid signal transmission and produce a wide range of neurological symptoms depending on which regions of the central nervous system or peripheral nervous system are affected.

Communication between neurons occurs at specialized junctions called synapses. At a chemical synapse, the electrical signal in the presynaptic neuron triggers the release of chemical messenger molecules called neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft, the narrow gap between the presynaptic and postsynaptic membranes. Neurotransmitters bind to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane, either exciting or inhibiting the postsynaptic neuron. Glutamate is the principal excitatory neurotransmitter in the central nervous system, while gamma-aminobutyric acid, commonly abbreviated as GABA, is the principal inhibitory neurotransmitter. Other important neurotransmitters include dopamine, serotonin, acetylcholine, and norepinephrine, each of which modulates neural activity in distinct circuits and plays roles in regulating mood, cognition, motor function, and autonomic physiology.

The blood-brain barrier is a specialized physiological structure that separates the circulating blood from the brain's extracellular fluid. It is formed primarily by the tight junctions between endothelial cells lining the blood vessels of the central nervous system, along with contributions from astrocytes and pericytes. The primary function of the blood-brain barrier is to protect the brain from potentially harmful substances in the bloodstream, including toxins, pathogens, and large molecules that could disrupt neural function. The blood-brain barrier allows certain molecules to cross selectively, including oxygen, glucose, and small lipid-soluble molecules, while excluding larger or more polar molecules. This selective permeability poses a significant challenge for drug delivery to the central nervous system, because many potentially therapeutic molecules are unable to cross the blood-brain barrier in sufficient concentrations to produce the desired effect.

Neuroplasticity refers to the ability of the nervous system to modify its structure and function in response to experience, injury, or disease. The nervous system undergoes profound changes throughout life, from the early developmental period through old age. During development, neurons form far more connections than will ultimately be retained, and a period of synaptic pruning eliminates connections that are not strengthened by activity. This activity-dependent refinement of neural circuits underlies the sensitive periods for the acquisition of language and other skills that are observed in early childhood. In adult life, neuroplasticity continues to support learning and memory through mechanisms including long-term potentiation, a persistent strengthening of synaptic connections that occurs when two neurons are repeatedly activated together.

Following injury to the central nervous system, neuroplasticity can support recovery of function through the reorganization of surviving neural circuits. After a stroke, for example, cortical areas adjacent to the damaged tissue may expand their representation of the body or reorganize to take over functions previously carried out by the injured area. The extent of recovery depends on many factors, including the size and location of the injury, the age of the patient, and the intensity of rehabilitation. Research into neuroplasticity continues to be a major focus of neuroscience, with the goal of developing interventions that harness the inherent adaptive capacity of the nervous system to promote recovery following brain injury or to slow cognitive decline in neurodegenerative diseases.

The nervous system develops from a thin sheet of embryonic tissue called the neural plate, which folds to form the neural tube during the third and fourth weeks of gestation. The neural tube eventually gives rise to the brain and the spinal cord. Defects in neural tube closure, which can result from nutritional deficiencies including folic acid deficiency, lead to conditions such as spina bifida and anencephaly. The neurons that make up the mature nervous system are generated from progenitor cells that line the ventricular zone of the developing neural tube. These neurons migrate to their final positions, guided by molecular cues in the developing tissue, and establish the characteristic layered structure of the cerebral cortex and other brain regions.

The human brain continues to develop throughout childhood and adolescence and into early adulthood. The prefrontal cortex, which is critical for executive function, decision-making, impulse control, and social cognition, is among the last regions to reach structural maturity, with myelination of prefrontal connections continuing into the mid-twenties. This prolonged developmental trajectory has implications for understanding adolescent risk-taking behavior, the vulnerability of the developing brain to environmental exposures including alcohol and other substances, and the onset of many psychiatric disorders during adolescence and early adulthood.

Research on the central nervous system has been transformed in recent decades by the development of new technologies for studying brain structure and function. Magnetic resonance imaging allows detailed visualization of brain anatomy in living subjects. Functional magnetic resonance imaging measures changes in blood oxygenation that accompany neural activity, providing an indirect measure of regional brain activation. Electroencephalography records the electrical activity of the brain through electrodes placed on the scalp and provides excellent temporal resolution of neural dynamics. Two-photon microscopy allows researchers to image individual neurons and their synaptic connections in the living brain with subcellular resolution. Single-cell RNA sequencing has enabled comprehensive characterization of the molecular diversity of cell types in the brain. Together, these technologies are accelerating progress toward a comprehensive understanding of how the nervous system gives rise to behavior, cognition, and consciousness.
